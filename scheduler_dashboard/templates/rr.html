Introduction to Round Robin

Round Robin (RR) is one of the oldest and most widely used CPU scheduling algorithms, designed specifically for time-sharing systems. Each process gets a small unit of CPU time called a time quantum (TQ) or time slice.

If a process doesn't complete within its time quantum, it is preempted (paused) and moved to the back of the ready queue. The next process then gets its turn. This continues in a circular fashion until all processes complete.

üí° Key Property:

Round Robin is preemptive and provides guaranteed response time. If there are n processes and the time quantum is q, each process gets 1/n of the CPU in chunks of at most q time units.

Choosing the Right Time Quantum

The time quantum is the most critical parameter in Round Robin. The wrong choice can severely impact performance:

‚ùå TQ Too Small

High context switch overhead

CPU spends time switching, not executing

Poor throughput

Many cache misses

‚ùå TQ Too Large

Degenerates to FCFS

Poor response time

Short jobs wait too long

Loses fairness benefits

Rule of Thumb:

Choose TQ so that about 80% of CPU bursts complete within one quantum. Typical values: 10-100 milliseconds in modern systems.

Step-by-Step Algorithm

1 Initialize Circular Queue

Create a circular ready queue. Set time quantum TQ.

2 Enqueue Arriving Processes

Add new processes to the rear of the queue as they arrive.

3 Dequeue Front Process

Take the process from the front of the queue.

4 Execute for TQ or Until Complete

Run for min(remaining_burst, TQ). Update remaining_burst.

5 Check Completion

If remaining_burst > 0, re-enqueue at rear. Otherwise, record completion time.

6 Repeat Until All Complete

Continue until queue is empty and all processes done.

Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Fair ‚Äî every process gets equal CPU time
  • No starvation ‚Äî guaranteed response time
  • Good for interactive systems
  • Simple to implement (circular queue)
  • Low response time for short processes
  • Works well with mixed workloads

Disadvantages

  • Context switch overhead
  • TQ selection is critical
  • Higher average TAT than SJF
  • No priority support
  • Not suitable for real-time
  • Performance depends heavily on TQ choice
